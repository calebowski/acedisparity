\part{Abstract}
Mass extinction events are known to generate profound reorganisations of the global biota.
From a macroevolutionary perspective, extinction events are destructive for the lineages that die out, but generative for survivors that are offered ecological opportunity in the form of vacant niches.
Adaptive radiation theory suggests that these surviving lineages undergo rapid taxonomic and morphological diversification.
% DONE:TG: I think that's a very quick jump to what adaptive radiationt theory suggests. 
The diversification of placental mammals after the Cretaceous-Palaeogene (K-Pg) mass extinction has been traditionally viewed as an archetypal adaptive radiation, with the extinction of the non-avian dinosaurs considered the catalyst for the rapid rise in eutherian diversity (placental mammals and extinct relatives). 
However, the last decade of discoveries in the fossil record and advances in comparative methods have revealed conflicting evidence surrounding the role of the K-Pg extinction in the taxonomic and morphological diversification of eutherians.
Recent studies have suggested an ecological radiation may have begun prior to the K-Pg event, while others indicate significant diversification occurring afterward.
These discrepancies in findings are likely rooted in contrasting methodologies when investigating morphological diversity (hereafter termed disparity).
% DONE TG: elaborate on what discrepancies 
The central research question of this project is how different methodological choices, such as the type of morphological data, the phylogenetic tree used and various downstream metrics and models applied, influence our understanding of the effects of the K-Pg event on eutherian  disparity. 
To investigate this, I aim to develop a pipeline that iterates through an extensive number of parameter combinations, reflecting the wide-ranging choices available to researchers studying disparity. 
Once completed, this pipeline will offer a comprehensive reproducible framework for studying the effects of mass extinctions on disparity, which will elucidate how the discrepancies in adaptive signals during the K-Pg event in previous studies may have been a result of contrasting methodological choices.
Furthermore, the pipeline can be a useful tool for the wider community to be applied to other clades and extinction events.

\section{Mass extinctions}
\subsection{What are they}

Extinction is a common process throughout Earth history; 99\% of species that have ever existed are now extinct \citep{overholt_biodiversity_crisis_2003}. 
However, extinction rates are not constant and occur in pulses across background taxonomic turnover \citep{raup_mass_1982}. 
Extinction events that wipe out over 75\% of species are termed `mass extinctions' and have occurred five times over the last 500 million years \citep{raup_mass_1982}. 
Mass extinctions drive a reorganisation of biodiversity as episodes of mass species turnover \citep{jablonski_lessons_2001}. 
On a macroevolutionary scale, mass extinctions can be considered both destructive and generative; derailing the evolutionary processes of selection and speciation that occur during periods of background extinction \citep{gould_structure_2002}, yet triggering adaptive radiations in the aftermath as unoccupied niches are filled by surviving clades \citep{losos_adaptive_2010, toljagic_triassicjurassic_2013}. 
Therefore, mass extinction boundaries often mark the most profound species composition transitions in Earth history.

\subsection{Recovery dynamics}
Classic adaptive radiation theory posits a linkage between taxonomic and phenotypic diversification, which are expected to follow a pattern of early high rates that decrease through time \citep{schluter2000ecology}.
However, empirical evidence often finds a decoupling of taxonomic richness and morphological diversity (termed disparity).
Peak taxonomic diversity and morphological disparity may be reached at different points in a clade's history, suggesting a discordance between these metrics of diversity \citep{foote_discordance_1993, hopkins2013decoupling, ruta2013decoupling,wan2021decoupling}.
This discordance has been observed across mass extinction events \citep{bapst_graptoloid_2012,edie_contrasting_2018,ezcurra_rise_2018}. 
Therefore, quantifying species composition turnover based solely on taxonomic diversity metrics such as species richness may not encapsulate the full complexity of recovery \citep{puttick_complex_2020}. 
Survivors filling the vacant niches left by extinct lineages allow this increase in ecological diversity.
Alternatively, surviving clades may proliferate without undergoing substantial ecological or functional change by persisting within pre-existing roles \citep{brusatte2008first, bapst_graptoloid_2012}.

\section{Morphology, Disparity and Extinction}
Analyses of morphology provide a key insights into past evolutionary processes, especially across major evolutionary events such as a mass extinction.
Disparity, the term used to describe morphological variation, has been widely used to investigate patterns of evolution in extant and extinct clades \citep{wills_disparity_1994}.
Disparity is often used as a way of describing the multidimensional `trait spaces' that organisms occupy.
\cite{guillerme_shifting_2020} outlines three ways these traitspaces can be described: size, density and position.
Disparity is often used as a proxy for ecological variation, particularly when the morphological traits are inferred to be correlated to ecological function.
Mass extinctions pose numerous scenarios of disparity (\autoref{fig:scenarios}); intuitively, mass extinctions may be expected to result in a decrease in disparity in tandem with selected removal of taxonomic diversity, as well as suppressed morphological evolution during recovery.
Alternatively, when extinction is random, disparity may be maintained. 
In adaptive scenarios, disparity may increase in opportunistic clades that diversify during the period of ecological opportunity following a mass extinction. 

Idea of extinction selectivity: Devonian events are selective, Permian was non-selective \citep{korn_extinction_2013}.

\subsection{Extinction selectivity}
Mass extinction events can have varying effects on the morphospace of clades.
By removing a subset of taxa, the distribution of taxa within the morphospace is transformed. 
Random extinction will reduce the total number of taxa, but have little effect on variance-based disparity metrics, whereas selective extinction, whereby taxa are more susceptible to extinction due to particular trait combinations, will have a detectable effect on variance-based measures \citep{foote1997evolution,korn_extinction_2013}.
\cite{korn_extinction_2013} proposed several patterns to describe different selection processes: lateral (asymmetric selection that trims one side of the morphospace), marginal (symmetric selection that removes peripheral taxa) and non-selective (random process independent of the variables defining the morphospace). 
In contemporary extinctions, this idea of `extinction space' is also valid \citep{ali2023bird,matthews2024global}.

\section{Ancestral state estimation}
\subsection{Application of ASE}
Ancestral state estimation (ASE) is the broad method of estimating trait value(s) in a set of common ancestors given a set of observed data for operational taxa of a phylogenetic tree \citep{schluter1997likelihood,cunningham1998reconstructing, yang2006computational,revell_ancestral_2024}.
Trait data may be continuous, such as body size, or discrete, such as diet type or presence/absence of a character.
The application of ASE is rooted in phylogenetic comparative methods, but also has applications in linguistics.
As put by \cite{swofford1992parsimony}, `character state reconstructions can provide a powerful mechanism for studying many facets of the evolutionary process. However, the zeal with which these techniques are sometimes advocated belies the complexity of the problem'.

Intuitively, ASE is appealing for those studying deep-time patterns. 
The fragmented nature of the fossil record can yield low sampling coverage at deeper points in a phylogeny and so estimation of node values can increase statistical power.
ASE deals with issue of ghost lineages, as brought up by Friedman 2009 and Brusatte 2011.

\subsection{Discrete characters}
Discrete characters are typically analysed using parsimony or likelihood approaches, although likelihood methods are generally preferred.
Likelihood approaches, such as Bayesian inference or Maximum likelihood, optimise solutions based on the tree topology, branch lengths and the model of character evolution.
Discrete characters are modelled using a continuous time Markov chain process, known as the M\textit{k} model \citep{pagel1994detecting, yang2006computational}. 
This process is specified by a $\textit{k} \times \textit{k}$ matrix, \textbf{Q}, which gives the transition rates (\textit{q}) between states for each character. 
There are a number of M\textit{k} models that can be fit to data. 
The `equal-rates model' (ER) is the simplest and estimates just one parameter, since it assumes all transitions occur with equal probability, equivalent to the Jukes-Cantor model of molecular evolution.
The assumptions of the ER model can be eased with the `symmetric' model (SYM): rate of change between any two character states is equal in the forwards and backwards direction, but may differ between different pairs of states.
The `all-rates-different' (ARD) model allows for a different transition rate in each direction, includes a total of $\textit{k} \times (\textit{k} - 1)$ parameters to estimate.
