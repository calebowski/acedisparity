\subsection{Rebound dynamics}
% TG: I think you can more logically link to the previous paragraph last sentence: "Previous paragraph: Entire branches are trimmed and new ones arise". "New paragraph first sentence: These resulting drastic turnover are ...".
The aftermath of a mass extinction has typically been described as `recovery', yet \cite{jablonski2025mass} describe this as a misnomer, since this implies a return to the prior pre-extinction state.
Instead, biodiversity rebounds are multifaceted and the response from the survivors of a mass extinction event may take several forms, transforming the post-extinction biota \citep{hull_life_2015}.
For example, trends observed in different clades pre-mass extinction may continue unperturbed, including escalations \citep{hansen1999effect} and declines \citep{raup1981extintion}.
Alternatively, the aftermath of a mass extinction can trigger diversification as surviving clades are offered ecological opportunity, filling the vacant niches left by extinct lineages.

Rebound dynamics can differ by the metric of diversity considered.
A discordance between taxonomic and morphological diversity, which can be considered a proxy of ecological diversity, is common in empirical macroevolutionary studies.
Intuitively, a diversifying clade would exhibit simultaneous taxonomic and phenotypic diversification, which are expected to follow a pattern of early high rates that decrease through time as niches become saturated \citep{schluter2000ecology}.
However, empirical evidence often finds that peak taxonomic and morphological diversity are reached at different points in a clade's history \citep{foote_discordance_1993, hopkins2013decoupling, ruta2013decoupling,wan2021decoupling}.
This discordance has been observed across mass extinction events, such as in graptolites across the end-Ordovician mass extinction \citep{bapst_graptoloid_2012}, or therapsids across the Permian-Triassic mass extinction \citep{ruta2013decoupling}.
It has been suggested that functional diversity may be density-independent across mass extinction events \citep{edie_contrasting_2018}.
Therefore, understanding the rebound dynamics of clades based solely on taxonomic diversity metrics may not encapsulate the full complexity of rebound dynamics \citep{puttick_complex_2020}.

Eutherian mammals prior to the K-Pg boundary have been traditionally considered depauperate, small sized and limited in ecological variation, which contrasts the diversity seen in extant placentals.
The widely held view until recently, known as \textit{the suppression hypothesis}, follows that the extinction of non-avian  dinosaurs released eutherian mammals from constraints of predation and competition, stimulating a radiation at the onset of the Cenozoic \citep{osborn_law_1902,alroy_fossil_1999,codron_ecological_2013,oleary_placental_2013}. 
The most significant morphological evidence for the suppression hypothesis in the fossil record concerns a shift in body size disparity \citep{alroy_fossil_1999}. 
Mesozoic mammals generally weighed less than 100 g and rarely over 5 kg \citep{benevento_early_2023}. 
The maximum body mass of mammals increased exponentially and by roughly three orders of magnitude by the late Eocene, which is consistent across continents and taxonomic groups \citep{cooper_body_2010,smith_evolution_2010,raia_rapid_2013,slater_phylogenetic_2013}. 

While the suppression hypothesis is strongly supported by studies that have investigated changes in body size \citep{alroy_fossil_1999,slater_phylogenetic_2013}, body size is limited as a biodiversity metric for ecological variation since it can be influenced by several factors that may reflect stochastic rather than adaptive changes \citep{grossnickle_feeding_2020}. 
Recent studies instead focusing on morpho-functional traits (intrinsically linked to ecological function), such as jaw and dentition morphology, have instead suggested that eutherian diversity was attained through successive waves of diversification, starting far earlier than the K-Pg mass extinction \citep{grossnickle_untangling_2019}.
The early rise hypothesis suggests an ecological diversification that occurred concurrently with the diversification of angiosperms and insects during and after the Cretaceous Terrestrial Revolution (125–80 Ma) (\autoref{fig:radiations}) \citep{meredith_impacts_2011,benton_angiosperm_2022}. 
Ecological variation within Mesozoic mammals is not limited to diet; diversity in locomotor styles was reached as far back as the Jurassic in early mammaliaform groups, including early eutherians \citep{chen_assembly_2019}. 
Other studies have pointed towards the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), an episode of elevated global temperatures 56 Ma that widened the latitudinal range of angiosperm-dominated forest, which in turn spurred the dispersal and radiation of eutherians (\autoref{fig:radiations}) \citep{bininda-emonds_delayed_2007}. 
\cite{benevento_patterns_2019} found that eutherian mammals had the most significant expansion in jaw morphospace at the onset of the Eocene.
While these alternative hypotheses do not necessarily refute the influence of the K-Pg mass extinction, they instead suggest the extinction accentuated evolutionary trajectories already underway.

\subsubsection{Types of trait data}
The morphological data used in disparity analyses may fall into one of the following categories:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Discrete characters}, i.e.~coding presence/absence or characteristic of traits. Discrete character matrices allow the inclusion of morphological features that are only present in a subset of the taxa. 
    These datasets are often originally collected for phylogenetics and repurposed for disparity.
    \item \textbf{Continuous characters}, i.e.~lengths of limbs, body mass, etc. More suited to investigations on a specific trait, rather than whole organism disparity.
    \item \textbf{Geometric morphometric}, i.e.~homologous landmark data (e.g. Procrustes coordinates). More common for analyses at lower taxonomic levels, since need for homologous features in all specimens.
\end{itemize}

Given the varying scope of morphological variation that these contrasting approaches characterise, it is expected that they capture different aspects of disparity. 
For example, molar geometric morphometric data will mainly reveal variation related to diet/feeding ecology, whereas discrete character matrices of cranial and postcranial features may reveal variation in whole-organism morphology. 
Correlation between the outputs of these two approaches cannot be assumed, highlighting the discrepancies between disparity analyses.
For example, \cite{halliday_eutherian_2016}, using a cladistic data matrix of dental, cranial and postcranial characters, found contrasting patterns of disparity across K-Pg compared to studies that used geometric morphometric analysis of molars \citep{wilson_mammals_2013,grossnickle_therian_2016}.
Furthermore, the overwhelming phylogenetic signal in cladistic discrete datasets, i.e. \cite{halliday_eutherian_2016}, may bury important functional variation contained within \citep{anderson2012using}. 
Characters used during phylogenetics are selected to establish branching orders and differentiate taxa into distinct clades, which may artificially inflate disparity at nodes of cladogenesis \citep{gerber_use_2019}.

Discrete characters are modelled using a continuous time Markov chain process, known as the M\textit{k} model (where \textit{k} = number of possible states) \citep{pagel1994detecting, yang2006computational}.

\end{bmatrix}

\]

There are a number of M\textit{k} transition rate models that can be fit to data. 
The `equal-rates model' (ER) is the simplest and estimates just one parameter, since it assumes all transitions occur with equal probability, equivalent to the Jukes-Cantor model of molecular evolution.
The assumptions of the ER model can be eased with the `symmetric' model (SYM): rate of change between any two character states is equal in the forwards and backwards direction, but may differ between different pairs of states.
The `all-rates-different' (ARD) model allows for a different transition rate in each direction, includes a total of $\textit{k} \times (\textit{k} - 1)$ parameters to estimate.

\subsubsection{Continuous characters}
Continuous trait evolution is typically modeled under a Brownian motion (BM) model of evolution \citep{felsenstein1985phylogenies, omeara2006testing}.
Brownian motion models trait evolution as a directionless, stochastic process under which continuous characters undergo changes along the branches of a phylogeny.
These evolutionary changes come from a Gaussian distribution with a mean of 0 and variance proportional to the product of the rate of evolution and time elapsed ($\sigma^2\textit{t}$). 
Therefore, the expected amount of trait divergence between any two taxa is proportional to the amount of shared evolutionary history according to the phylogeny.

Under BM, the vector (\textbf{x}) of observed tip trait values follows a multivariate normal (MVN) distribution and has an expected value that is equal to the root state $x_0$.
\[
\boldsymbol{x} \sim \mathrm{MVN}(x_{0},\, \sigma^2 \boldsymbol{C})
\]
\textbf{\textit{C}} is a variance-covariance matrix of N dimensions where the diagonal elements $\boldsymbol{C}_{ii}$ are the tree heights from root to tip \textit{i} and $\boldsymbol{C}_{ij}$ is the height above the root of the most recent common ancestor of \textit{i} and \textit{j}. 
When estimating ancestral states under BM, the trait values of the internal nodes are also distributed within this MVN distribution.
Therefore, the \textbf{\textit{C}} matrix can be expanded by one column and row for each node (except the root).
During Maximum Likelihood, the $x_0$ and $\sigma^2$ parameters are estimated as the values that maximize the likelihood of observing the tip vector. 
Internal node values can be estimated using the conditional expectations of this distribution, i.e. the trait value at an internal node is calculated as a weighted average of the observed tip values, determined by the amount of shared evolutionary history (given by \textbf{\textit{C}}) and the node's position in the tree.

\textit{`Character state reconstructions can provide a powerful mechanism for studying many facets of the evolutionary process. However, the zeal with which these techniques are sometimes advocated belies the complexity of the problem'} \citep{swofford1992parsimony}.

\subsubsection{Uncertainty in estimates}
A critical issue within ancestral state estimation is that estimates tend to have broad uncertainty \citep{schluter1997likelihood}.
This uncertainty is not uniform across the tree: it typically increases towards root of the tree, where direct information from observed tip data becomes increasingly diffuse \citep{gascuel2014predicting}.
For discrete characters, this uncertainty manifests as inconclusive scaled likelihoods, i.e., state 0 = 0.51, state 1 = 0.49.
For continuous characters, uncertainty is wide confidence intervals.

How this uncertainty in estimations should be dealt with is equivocal.
Despite the encouragement for uncertainty to be carried forward in comparative analyses \citep{silvestro2015measurement}, most computational implementations of ASE return point estimates.
For continuous characters, this will be the median value of the confidence interval and for discrete characters, it will be the most probable state according to a threshold (see later).
By ignoring uncertainty, point estimates can lead to overconfidence in results and erroneous inferences in downstream analyses (such as down the disparity pipeline), all while obscuring potentially `accurate' uncertainty.

For discrete characters, there is a level of subjectivity in how to collapse the states into the most probable based on their scaled likelihood.
It is common to have a threshold implementation in ASE software, whereby the user can set a scaled likelihood at which point states are collapsed to a point estimate, or left as uncertain, which is denoted as `0/1', for example.
However, these thresholds are often arbitrarily set with no guidance of what is the `correct' value \citep{smith2022evolution,casali2023morphological}.
Much of the subjectivity relates to the trade-off between permissiveness and conservativeness; higher thresholds are likely too conservative and so too many states are left uncertain, perhaps missing true signals.
Whereas lower thresholds may be too permissive and can lead to erroneous inferences.
Interestingly, \cite{keating2023best} highlights that uncertainty returned by a specified model is a better predictor of accuracy than likelihood diagnostics, such as AIC.

\subsubsection{Sensitivity to fossil sampling}
Given that uncertainty is often high in ASE, how then can estimates be improved?
\cite{ane2008analysis} demonstrates that ASE is \textit{statistically inconsistent}, meaning that even if the sample size of extant tips is infinite, estimates are not expected to converge on the true value.
Instead, the inclusion of fossils can improve estimates, due to the shorter evolutionary path to the root of the tree \citep{finarelli2006ancestral,finarelli2013potential}.
Fossils can be used as priors on nodes, or simply added to the tree as tips.

By extension, inclusion of fossils can greatly influence inferences into disparity.
Extant-only datasets often miss early rapid morphological diversification signals \citep{harmon2010early}, whereas analyses incorporating fossil data often finds the reverse \citep{hughes2013clades}
For example,~\cite{mitchell2015extant} found that analyses of bird disparity were underestimated when using ancestral state estimation of extant-only datasets.

\subsubsection{Model dependency}
Another key challenge with ASE is its sensitivity to violations of its model assumptions.
For continuous characters, this is particularly pertinent when traits have evolved under non-BM \citep{revell2025ancestral}.
When trait evolution deviates from BM—due to directional trends, bounded evolution, or regime shifts—ASE can return biased estimations of ancestral states.

Both \cite{webster2002testing} and \cite{finarelli2006ancestral} were unable to recover accurate ancestral estimates of body size in planktonic foraminifera and mammals, respectively, due to a trend of increase through time.
This violates the assumption of zero-mean change under BM.
Similar issues arise when the trait data evolved under an Ornstein Uhlenbeck model, whereby trait data evolves in a Brownian way but under a stabilizing selection \citep{royer2016comparison}.

It is common for regime shifts to occur at mass extinction boundaries \citep{jablonski1986background}.
For example, \cite{slater_phylogenetic_2013} found that mammal body size was relatively constrained (OU) up until the K-Pg mass extinction which then allowed a release of constraint and Brownian motion evolution.



As discussed previously, fossil sampling is crucial for accurate ASE, especially when model assumptions are violated \citep{puttick2016partially}.
Fossils assist in estimating and constraining model parameters, as well as distinguishing different evolutionary processes \citep{goswami2025phenomics}. 
For example, when estimating states under an OU model, fossils are necessary for estimating the primary optimum.
Similarly, fossils would be necessary to detect a trend, such as Cope's rule.
A similar logic applies to discrete characters.

\cite{keating2023best} found that inclusion of fossils improved ASE estimates, irrespective of model misspecification.

\subsubsection{Pre- or post-ordination}
ASE can be performed at two points in the disparity analysis pipeline (\autoref{fig:pipeline}): either pre-ordination or post-ordination. 
Pre-ordination ASE involves estimating the ancestral states of the raw trait data, whereas post-ordination ASE estimates the PC scores of ancestral nodes.
The pre-ordination has become favoured \citep{lloyd2018journeys} due to various statistical issues with post-ordination ASE.
For example, post-ordination ASE means ancestral values have to fall within the convex hull of the observed trait data \citep{stayton2015definition}.
While this is usually biologically realistic due to the zero-force evolutionary law \citep{mcshea2010biology}, in a scenario where taxon sampling is poor, true ancestral states may fall outside the morphological extremes designated by observed data.

\cite{lloyd2018journeys} found that the post-ordination ASE method recovers an implausibly low amount of convergent evolution and patterns dictated more so by phylogenetic signal than morphological signal.

Despite this, there are some practical benefits to performing post-ordination ASE.
Firstly, it is less computationally expensive.
Secondly, it mitigates issues with trait correlations. 
ASE methods assume trait independence, which is rare in morphological trait datasets (e.g., body size and limb length are positively correlated).  
Pre-ordination ASE, by ignoring this correlation, may return biologically implausible estimations that violate allometry, for example.
Through ordination, the dominant sources of trait correlation are captured within the first few PC axes.
The orthogonality of PC axes means zero co-variance between `traits' (which are now PC axes) thus reducing the violation of the assumption of independence.

\subsection{Introduction}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Morphology can tell us interesting patterns about evolutionary processes.
    \item The combination of morphological datasets (using fossils) and phylogenies means we can infer these processes through deep time
    \item This is particularly useful when studying major evolutionary events (such as a mass extinction)
    \item However, the fossil record is incomplete and this restricts our ability to look at deep time patterns of morphology.
    \item Many studies have made use of ancestral state estimation (ASE) to fill in gaps left by `ghost lineages'. We know some species must have existed between root and tips but we don't know what they looked like.
    \item Particularly has been implemented in studies looking at disparity, which describes the multidimensional `morphospace' in which taxa occupy.
    \item ASE can be used to estimate the character state values (both continuous and discrete characters) at nodes on a phylogeny, using observed trait data (from living and fossil specimens).
\end{itemize}

    \item Erroneous estimates can mislead values of disparity and therefore macroevolutionary inferences.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Simulations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item I then ran ASE on these matrices using \texttt{multi.ace}, with three different parameters (for discrete states). 
    \item \textbf{Relative majority rule}: the ancestral state estimated are all the ones that are have a scaled likelihood greater than the maximum observed scaled likelihood minus the inverse number of possible states: \[
        \text{estimated~state} \geq \left( \max(\text{likelihood}) - \frac{1}{n_{\text{states}}} \right) %this formula is wrong.
    \]  If more than one state is over this threshold, it is recovered as uncertain e.g., (0/1).    
    \item \textbf{Strict majority rule}: Setting threshold parameter to a set value will select the ancestral states estimate with a scaled likelihood equal or greater than this designated value (in this case I did 0.5).
    \item \textbf{No majority rule (sampling)}: \texttt{multi.ace} has a \texttt{sample = x} parameter, whereby you can return x matrices, in which states returned are by the scaled likelihood of each state appearing at each node. In this case I used sample = 10 for fast computation (in future I will do 100/1000).
