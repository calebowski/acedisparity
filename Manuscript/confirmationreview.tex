\part{Abstract}
Mass extinction events are known to generate profound reorganisations of the global biota.
From a macroevolutionary perspective, extinction events are destructive for the lineages that die out, but generative for survivors that are offered ecological opportunity in the form of vacant niches.
Adaptive radiation theory suggests that these surviving lineages undergo rapid taxonomic and morphological diversification.
% DONE:TG: I think that's a very quick jump to what adaptive radiationt theory suggests. 
The diversification of placental mammals after the Cretaceous-Palaeogene (K-Pg) mass extinction has been traditionally viewed as an archetypal adaptive radiation, with the extinction of the non-avian dinosaurs considered the catalyst for the rapid rise in eutherian diversity (placental mammals and extinct relatives). 
However, the last decade of discoveries in the fossil record and advances in comparative methods have revealed conflicting evidence surrounding the role of the K-Pg extinction in the taxonomic and morphological diversification of eutherians.
Recent studies have suggested an ecological radiation may have begun prior to the K-Pg event, while others indicate significant diversification occurring afterward.
These discrepancies in findings are likely rooted in contrasting methodologies when investigating morphological diversity (hereafter termed disparity).

The central research question of this project is how different methodological choices, such as the type of morphological data, the phylogenetic tree used and various downstream metrics and models applied, influence our understanding of the effects of the K-Pg event on eutherian  disparity. 
To investigate this, I aim to develop a pipeline that iterates through an extensive number of parameter combinations, reflecting the wide-ranging choices available to researchers studying disparity. 
Once completed, this pipeline will offer a comprehensive reproducible framework for studying the effects of mass extinctions on disparity, which will elucidate how the discrepancies in adaptive signals during the K-Pg event in previous studies may have been a result of contrasting methodological choices.
Furthermore, the pipeline can be a useful tool for the wider community to be applied to other clades and extinction events.

\section{Mass extinctions}
\subsection{What are they}

Extinction is a common process throughout Earth history; 99\% of species that have ever existed are now extinct \citep{overholt_biodiversity_crisis_2003}. 
However, extinction rates are not constant and occur in pulses across background taxonomic turnover \citep{raup_mass_1982}. 
Extinction events that wipe out over 75\% of species are termed `mass extinctions' and have occurred five times over the last 500 million years \citep{raup_mass_1982}. 
Mass extinctions drive a reorganisation of biodiversity as episodes of mass species turnover \citep{jablonski_lessons_2001}. 
On a macroevolutionary scale, mass extinctions can be considered both destructive and generative; derailing the evolutionary processes of selection and speciation that occur during periods of background extinction \citep{gould_structure_2002}, yet triggering adaptive radiations in the aftermath as unoccupied niches are filled by surviving clades \citep{losos_adaptive_2010, toljagic_triassicjurassic_2013}. 
Therefore, mass extinction boundaries often mark the most profound species composition transitions in Earth history.

\subsection{Recovery dynamics}
Classic adaptive radiation theory posits a linkage between taxonomic and phenotypic diversification, which are expected to follow a pattern of early high rates that decrease through time \citep{schluter2000ecology}.
However, empirical evidence often finds a decoupling of taxonomic richness and morphological diversity (termed disparity).
Peak taxonomic diversity and morphological disparity may be reached at different points in a clade's history, suggesting a discordance between these metrics of diversity \citep{foote_discordance_1993, hopkins2013decoupling, ruta2013decoupling,wan2021decoupling}.
This discordance has been observed across mass extinction events \citep{bapst_graptoloid_2012,edie_contrasting_2018,ezcurra_rise_2018}. 
Therefore, quantifying species composition turnover based solely on taxonomic diversity metrics such as species richness may not encapsulate the full complexity of recovery \citep{puttick_complex_2020}. 
Survivors filling the vacant niches left by extinct lineages allow this increase in ecological diversity.
Alternatively, surviving clades may proliferate without undergoing substantial ecological or functional change by persisting within pre-existing roles \citep{brusatte2008first, bapst_graptoloid_2012}.

\section{Morphology, Disparity and Extinction}
Analyses of morphology provide a key insights into past evolutionary processes, especially across major evolutionary events such as a mass extinction.
Disparity, the term used to describe morphological variation, has been widely used to investigate patterns of evolution in extant and extinct clades \citep{wills_disparity_1994}.
Disparity is often used as a way of describing the multidimensional `trait spaces' that organisms occupy.
\cite{guillerme_shifting_2020} outlines three ways these traitspaces can be described: size, density and position.
Disparity is often used as a proxy for ecological variation, particularly when the morphological traits are inferred to be correlated to ecological function.
Mass extinctions pose numerous scenarios of disparity (\autoref{fig:scenarios}); intuitively, mass extinctions may be expected to result in a decrease in disparity in tandem with selected removal of taxonomic diversity, as well as suppressed morphological evolution during recovery.
Alternatively, when extinction is random, disparity may be maintained. 
In adaptive scenarios, disparity may increase in opportunistic clades that diversify during the period of ecological opportunity following a mass extinction. 

Idea of extinction selectivity: Devonian events are selective, Permian was non-selective \citep{korn_extinction_2013}.

\subsection{Extinction selectivity}
Mass extinction events can have varying effects on the morphospace of clades.
By removing a subset of taxa, the distribution of taxa within the morphospace is transformed. 
Random extinction will reduce the total number of taxa, but have little effect on variance-based disparity metrics, whereas selective extinction, whereby taxa are more susceptible to extinction due to particular trait combinations, will have a detectable effect on variance-based measures \citep{foote1997evolution,korn_extinction_2013}.
\cite{korn_extinction_2013} proposed several patterns to describe different selection processes: lateral (asymmetric selection that trims one side of the morphospace), marginal (symmetric selection that removes peripheral taxa) and non-selective (random process independent of the variables defining the morphospace). 
In contemporary extinctions, this idea of `extinction space' is also valid \citep{ali2023bird,matthews2024global}.

\section{Ancestral state estimation}
\subsection{Application of ASE}
Ancestral state estimation (ASE) is the broad method of estimating trait value(s) in a set of common ancestors given a set of observed data for operational taxa of a phylogenetic tree \citep{schluter1997likelihood,cunningham1998reconstructing, yang2006computational,revell_ancestral_2024}.
Trait data may be continuous, such as body size, or discrete, such as diet type or presence/absence of a character.
The application of ASE is rooted in phylogenetic comparative methods, but also has applications in linguistics.
As put by \cite{swofford1992parsimony}, `character state reconstructions can provide a powerful mechanism for studying many facets of the evolutionary process. However, the zeal with which these techniques are sometimes advocated belies the complexity of the problem'.

Intuitively, ASE is appealing for those studying deep-time patterns of disparity.
% The fragmented nature of the fossil record can yield low sampling coverage at deeper points in a phylogeny and so estimation of node values can increase statistical power.
The fragmented nature of the fossil record means that means much of the phylogeny is left unsampled when including only observed taxa.
Incorporating a phylogeny into disparity analysis will unavoidably introduce unsampled `ghost lineages' \citep{brusatte2011phylogenetic}.
A ghost lineage is the hypothetical ancestor that is not observed in the fossil record, but can be inferred to have existed due to the presence of other fossils/extant taxa. 
This can be best explained as the branch on a phylogeny between two observed taxonomic units (see below)

ASE has been employed as a way of inferring the traits of these hypothetical species based on the traits of their descendents.
This fills in the gaps in the fossil record and alleviates the bias associated with uneven sampling across the phylogenetic tree \citep{friedman2009ecomorphological, brusatte2011phylogenetic}. 
When using ASE, the estimated traits for nodes of a phylogeny are used as a way of sampling these ghost lineages.
As detailed below, modern ASE methods make use of branch lengths to describe the temporal range over which morphological evolution has taken place.
Thus, longer ghost lineages can increase uncertainty in ASE estimates, which highlights the need for fossil sampling.
The importance of accounting for ghost lineages has been stressed, since it can impact our understand of the rate of morphological evolution.
Ignoring ghost lineages may find abrupt shifts in disparity that may be misleading of a more gradual accumulation that has been missed due to incomplete fossil sampling.
Therefore, using ASE tends to smooth the disparity curves through time.

\subsubsection{Discrete characters}
Discrete characters are typically analysed using parsimony or likelihood approaches, although likelihood methods are generally preferred.
Likelihood approaches, such as Bayesian inference or Maximum likelihood, optimise solutions based on the tree topology, branch lengths and the model of character evolution.
Discrete characters are modelled using a continuous time Markov chain process, known as the M\textit{k} model (where \textit{k} = number of possible states) \citep{pagel1994detecting, yang2006computational}. 
This process is specified by a $\textit{k} \times \textit{k}$ matrix, \textbf{Q}, which gives the instantaneous transition rates (\textit{q}) between states for each character. 

\[
\textbf{Q} = \begin{bmatrix}
    -q_{0,1} & q_{0,1} \\
    q_{1,0} & -q_{1,0}
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
    -0.5 & 0.5 \\
    0.5 & -0.5
\end{bmatrix}
\]

There are a number of M\textit{k} transition rate models that can be fit to data. 
The `equal-rates model' (ER) is the simplest and estimates just one parameter, since it assumes all transitions occur with equal probability, equivalent to the Jukes-Cantor model of molecular evolution.
The assumptions of the ER model can be eased with the `symmetric' model (SYM): rate of change between any two character states is equal in the forwards and backwards direction, but may differ between different pairs of states.
The `all-rates-different' (ARD) model allows for a different transition rate in each direction, includes a total of $\textit{k} \times (\textit{k} - 1)$ parameters to estimate.


This likelihood approach makes use of branch lengths, which is why it has become the preferred method of ASE compared to previous methods, such as parsimony. 
The probability of each transition at any given interval of time (\textit{t}) can be calculated by 

\[\textbf{P} = \exp(\textbf{Q}\textit{t})\]

So if we want to calculate probability of each transition at node AB (ancestor of A and B) \textit{t} = 0.3.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width = 10cm]{images/phylo_tree.png}
\end{figure}


\[
\textbf{P}_{t=0.4} = \exp(\textbf{Q} \times 0.4) = \begin{bmatrix}
        0.835 & 0.164  \\
        0.164 & 0.835
\end{bmatrix}
\]

One further probability to consider is the `root prior', which is essentially whether the root node of the tree was in state 0 or 1.
This is typically set with a flat root prior $\pi_{0} = \pi_{1} = 0.5$.

The most popular method of ASE is \textit{marginal} estimation, which involves proceeding from node to node on the phylogeny, and, at each node, computing the probability of observing the tip data of our tree conditioned on fixing the node we've visited to each one of the set of distinct values of our trait.
If we were to compute the probability that the root was in state 0, 

For example, if we were to calculate the probability of the root node was in state 0: \[
\begin{aligned}
P(\text{root} = 0) &= \pi_{0} \times P(0 | 0, t_{AB}) \times P(1 | 0, t_{A}) \times P(0 | 0, t_{B}) \times P(0 | 0, t_{C}) \\
&\quad + \pi_{0} \times P(1 | 0, t_{AB}) \times P(1 | 1, t_{A}) \times P(0 | 1, t_{B}) \times P(0 | 0, t_{C}) \\
% &= 0.5 \times 0.0434 + 0.5 \times 0.0035 \\
% &= 0.0234
\end{aligned}
\]

This is repeated for \textit{P}(\text{root} = 1) and then the likelihoods are rescaled by their sum so they add up to 1.0. 
In marginal estimation, this is calculated node by node.
Since this can become extremely complicated as the phylogeny increases in size, in practice functions use algorithms such as Felsenstein's pruning algorithm \citep{felsenstein1981evolutionary}.


\subsubsection{Continuous characters}
Continuous trait evolution is typically modelled under a Brownian motion (BM) model of evolution \citep{felsenstein1985phylogenies, omeara2006testing}.
Brownian motion models trait evolution as a directionless, stochastic process under which continuous characters undergo changes along the branches of a phylogeny.
These evolutionary changes come from a Gaussian distribution with a mean of 0 and variance proportional to the product of the rate of evolution and time elapsed ($\sigma^2\textit{t}$). 
Therefore, the expected amount of trait divergence between any two taxa is proportional to the amount of shared evolutionary history according to the phylogeny.

Under BM, the vector (\textbf{x}) of observed tip trait values follows a multivariate normal (MVN) distribution and has an expected value that is equal to the root state $x_0$.
\[
\boldsymbol{x} \sim \mathrm{MVN}(x_{0},\, \sigma^2 \boldsymbol{C})
\]
\textbf{\textit{C}} is an variance-covariance matrix of N dimensions where the diagonal elements $\boldsymbol{C}_{ii}$ accelerated the tree height from root to tip \textit{i} and $\boldsymbol{C}_{ij}$ is the height above the root of the most recent common ancestor of \textit{i} and \textit{j}. 
When estimating ancestral states under BM, the trait values of the internal nodes are also distributed within this MVN distribution.
Therefore, the \textbf{\textit{C}} matrix can be expanded by one column and row for each node (except the root).
During Maximum Likelihood, the $x_0$ and $\sigma^2$ parameters are estimated as the values that maximize the likelihood of observing the tip vector. 
Internal node values can be estimated using the conditional expectations of this distribution, i.e. the trait value at an internal node is calculated as a weighted average of the observed tip values, determined by the amount of shared evolutionary history (given by \textbf{\textit{C}}) and the node's position in the tree.

It is now consensus that the inclusion of fossils in macroevolutionary studies is necessary for recovering robust findings. 
This is especially true for ASE.
As previously detailed, uncertainty in ancestral state estimates increases at deeper nodes and so the inclusion of fossils, particularly closer to the root, improves the accuracy of estimates by breaking branch lengths \citep{finarelli2006ancestral,finarelli2013potential}.
The importance of fossil inclusion is made even more pertinent when temporal heterogeneity or a mass extinction event means extant data is not representative of earlier ancestral states.
For example, if a clade evolved under a BM trend model (a moving average), current implementations of ASE assume a fixed mean, which, if given only extant taxa, will find a mean skewed towards the tips rather than root. 
Both \cite{webster2002testing} and \cite{finarelli2006ancestral} were unable to recover accurate ancestral estimates of body size in planktonic foraminifera and mammals, respectively, due to a trend of increase through time.
Since ancestral states are limited to what is observed, and we know that trait distributions can change dramatically through time, fossils provide the most reliable evidence of the trait distribution at that point.

The necessity of fossil inclusion extends to disparity analyses too.
\cite{mitchell2015extant} found that analyses of bird disparity were underestimated when using extant-only datasets.

\subsection{Introduction}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Morphology can tell us interesting patterns about evolutionary processes.
    \item The combination of morphological datasets (using fossils) and phylogenies means we can infer these processes through deep time
    \item This is particularly useful when studying major evolutionary events (such as a mass extinction)
    \item However, the fossil record is incomplete and this restricts our ability to look at deep time patterns of morphology.
    \item Many studies have made use of ancestral state estimation (ASE) to fill in gaps left by `ghost lineages'. We know some species must have existed between root and tips but we don't know what they looked like.
    \item Particularly has been implemented in studies looking at disparity, which describes the multidimensional `morphospace' in which taxa occupy.
    \item ASE can be used to estimate the character state values (both continuous and discrete characters) at nodes on a phylogeny, using observed trait data (from living and fossil specimens).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Why does this matter?}
\begin{itemize}
    \item ASE estimates are often shit and a `glorified average'. But, what can we do with them?
    \item Erroneous estimates can mislead values of disparity and therefore macroevolutionary inferences.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Methods}
\subsubsection{Simulations}
\begin{itemize}
    \item I simulated a set of 100 trees using \texttt{treats} \citep{guillerme2024treats}. These trees were simulated with birth-death parameters as $\lambda = 1.0$, and $\mu = 0.7$. This leaves trees with a range of 50-200 fossil tips and 50 extant tips.
    \item I simulated discrete and continuous trait data using \texttt{map.traits}. This effectively maps the 'true' evolutionary history across the phylogeny (tips and nodes have states mapped).
    \item For discrete traits, I simulated trait data across each tree (100 characters, 85\% binary, 15\% 3-state) using M\textit{k} model with 3 different transition rates: fast = 0.9, medium = 0.1, slow = 0.01.
    \item For continuous traits, I simulated BM and OU model trait data (50 characters).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Fossil sampling}
\begin{itemize}
    \item I then removed all node states from the matrices, leaving just tip states.
    \item In order to understand how ASE accuracy is affected by fossil sampling level, I pruned the fossil tips at 5 levels, preserving the root to maintain tree height across each level:
    \item \textbf{Living}: only extant tips are retained
    \item \textbf{Low}: 5\% fossils retained
    \item \textbf{Medium}: 15\% fossils retained
    \item \textbf{High}: 50\% fossils retained
    \item \textbf{All}: All fossils retained
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{ASE}
\begin{itemize}
    \item I then ran ASE on these matrices using \texttt{multi.ace}, with three different parameters (for discrete states). 
    \item \textbf{Relative majority rule}: the ancestral state estimated are all the ones that are have a scaled likelihood greater than the maximum observed scaled likelihood minus the inverse number of possible states: \[
        \text{estimated~state} \geq \left( \max(\text{likelihood}) - \frac{1}{n_{\text{states}}} \right) %this formula is wrong.
    \]  If more than one state is over this threshold, it is recovered as uncertain e.g., (0/1).    
    \item \textbf{Strict majority rule}: Setting threshold parameter to a set value will select the ancestral states estimate with a scaled likelihood equal or greater than this designated value (in this case I did 0.5).
    \item \textbf{No majority rule (sampling)}: \texttt{multi.ace} has a \texttt{sample = x} parameter, whereby you can return x matrices, in which states returned are by the scaled likelihood of each state appearing at each node. In this case I used sample = 10 for fast computation (in future I will do 100/1000).
    \item Ran \texttt{multi.ace} on continuous characters using BM as model.
    \item I will also do post-ordination ace to see how that compares to pre-ordination ace.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Raw distance comparison between true vs ace}

    \item Distances were calculated only for nodes that were present in all fossil sampling levels to allow for comparison.

    \item For continuous traits, I firstly scaled each matrix and then measured the euclidean distance between true vs ace for each corresponding node.
    \item I also looked at the distance at just the root, to see how well ace recovers true value at deepest node.

    \item At the root node, the impact of fossil sampling becomes more pertinent i.e., higher fossil sampling improves estimates
    \item For continuous traits, ASE accuracy was much better for BM traits than OU
    \item Estimates of continuous traits stay relatively constant across fossil preservation levels.
    \item For continuous traits, using point estimates was more accurate than sample method.
\end{itemize} 

\subsubsection{Ordination}
\begin{itemize}
    \item I expect that patterns of distance to true value will correspond to the raw distance pattern (more fossil sampling = higher ASE accuracy, sample method = higher ASE accuracy)
    \item I expect that fast transition rates will exhibit clustering at the centre of the morphospace, particularly with the default multi.ace parameter, due to the high amount of uncertainty recovered at deeper nodes. I think that the sample method of ASE will alleviate this artefact by removing this uncertainty.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Disparity}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Preliminary results have found that using sample ASE method recovers high accuracy for all transition rates.
    \item Using the default ASE method recovers much lower accuracy, particularly with low fossil sampling.
    \item I expect to see different results for different disparity metrics. For example, metrics that focus on density will be able to identify these artifacts of clustering due to uncertainty in node estimates, which variance based metrics will miss.
    \item This will highlight the need for multiple different disparity metrics to be used in disparity analyses
\end{itemize}
