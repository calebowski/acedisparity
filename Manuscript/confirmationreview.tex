\section{Morphology, Disparity and Extinction}
\subsection{Mass extinctions}
% \subsection{What are they}
Extinction is a fundamental evolutionary process that, alongside speciation, continuously shapes patterns of biodiversity through deep time; over 99\% of all species that have ever lived are now extinct \citep{raup1981extintion}.
However, extinction rates are not constant and occur in pulses across background taxonomic turnover \citep{raup_mass_1982}. 
\textit{Mass extinctions} are specific events that wipe out over 75\% of species and have happened five times over the last 500 million years \citep{raup_mass_1982}. 
% On a macroevolutionary scale, mass extinctions can be considered both destructive and generative; derailing the evolutionary processes of selection and speciation that occur during periods of background extinction \citep{gould_structure_2002} and triggering  \citep{losos_adaptive_2010, toljagic_triassicjurassic_2013}. 
On a macroevolutionary scale, mass extinctions mark the most dramatic species composition transitions in Earth history \citep{jablonski_lessons_2001}.
Entire branches are trimmed from the tree of life, while the evolutionary trajectories of survivors are altered in the wake of a transformed global biota \citep{hull_life_2015}. %TG: this sentence is a bit "flourished" and contrasts with the other previous ones. \subsection{Rebound dynamics}
The aftermath of this drastic species turnover is typically described as `recovery', yet \cite{jablonski2025mass} describe this as a misnomer, since this implies a return to the prior pre-extinction state.
Instead, biodiversity rebounds are multifaceted and the response from the survivors of a mass extinction event may take several forms, transforming the post-extinction biota \citep{hull_life_2015}.
For example, trends observed in different clades pre-mass extinction may continue unperturbed, including escalations \citep{hansen1999effect} and declines \citep{raup1981extintion}.
Alternatively, the aftermath of a mass extinction can trigger diversification as surviving clades are offered ecological opportunity, filling the vacant niches left by extinct lineages.

Rebound dynamics can differ by the metric of diversity considered.
A discordance between taxonomic and morphological diversity, which can be considered a proxy of ecological diversity, is common in empirical macroevolutionary studies.
Intuitively, a diversifying clade would exhibit simultaneous taxonomic and phenotypic diversification, which are expected to follow a pattern of early high rates that decrease through time as niches become saturated \citep{schluter2000ecology}.
However, empirical evidence often finds that peak taxonomic and morphological diversity are reached at different points in a clade's history \citep{foote_discordance_1993, hopkins2013decoupling, ruta2013decoupling,wan2021decoupling}.
This discordance has been observed across mass extinction events, such as in graptolites across the end-Ordovician mass extinction \citep{bapst_graptoloid_2012}, or therapsids across the Permian-Triassic mass extinction \citep{ruta2013decoupling}.
It has been suggested that functional diversity may be density-independent across mass extinction events \citep{edie_contrasting_2018}.
Therefore, understanding the rebound dynamics of clades based solely on taxonomic diversity metrics may not encapsulate the full complexity of rebound dynamics \citep{puttick_complex_2020}.

\subsection{Disparity}
This phenotypic diversity, often called \textit{disparity}, has been widely used to investigate patterns of evolution in extant and extinct clades \citep{wills_disparity_1994}.
Disparity refers to the extent of morphological diversity among taxa, typically quantified as the distribution or spread of those taxa within a morphospace — a multidimensional mathematical space configured by morphological similarity (where similar looking species are positioned closer together) \citep{guillerme_disparities_2020}.

Changes in morphospace through time are often closely tied to shifts in ecological diversity \citep{lowery2019morphospace,budd2021morphospace}. 
For example, expansion in morphospace may indicate a clade expanding its niche breadth during an adaptive radiation \citep{foote1994morphological,raup1994role,sidlauskas2008continuous}.
Alternatively, a clade may move position within the morphospace, reflecting shifts in ecological niche \citep{hopkins2016magnitude}.

\subsection{Extinction selectivity}
Mass extinctions pose numerous scenarios of disparity (\autoref{fig:scenarios}).
In adaptive scenarios, disparity may increase in clades that diversify during the period of ecological opportunity following a mass extinction due to morphospace expansion.
Extinction can also be random or selective based on specific traits, or regions of the morphospace, that go extinct.

\subsection{Disparity metric}
These different types of extinction events can be distinguished from each other by comparing changes in different disparity metrics.
For example, a variance-based metric such as sum of variances may remain relatively unchanged during a random extinction, since no systematic reduction in trait variance has occurred \citep{foote1997evolution,korn_extinction_2013}.
In contrast, a reduction in sum of variances will likely be apparent in a marginal selective extinction, where the pruning of peripheral taxa has led to a contraction in variance.
\cite{puttick_complex_2020} highlight the flaws in using a single disparity metric for assessing morphospace changes, since changes can occur in size, position and density \citep{guillerme_shifting_2020}.
Studies that have implemented multiple metrics have been able to identify nuanced changes in morphospace \citep{cole_selectivity_2021}.

\section{Eutherian disparity across K-Pg Mass Extinction}
\subsection{Suppression hypothesis}
The most recent of the `Big Five' mass extinctions occurred 66 million years ago (Ma) at the Cretaceous-Palaeogene (K-Pg) boundary, primarily known for the demise of the non-avian dinosaurs and ammonites \citep{alvarez_extraterrestrial_1980,schulte_chicxulub_2010,brusatte_extinction_2015}. 
The effect of the K-Pg mass extinction on eutherian mammals (placentals and extinct relatives) is one of the most well-studied yet contentious inquiries of macroevolutionary biology.
Eutherian mammals prior to the K-Pg boundary have been widely considered depauperate, small sized and limited in ecological variation, which contrasts the diversity seen in extant placentals.
This traditional view, known as the \textit{suppression hypothesis}, follows that the extinction of non-avian  dinosaurs released eutherian mammals from constraints of predation and competition, stimulating a radiation at the onset of the Cenozoic \citep{osborn_law_1902,alroy_fossil_1999,codron_ecological_2013,oleary_placental_2013}. 
The most significant morphological evidence for the suppression hypothesis in the fossil record concerns a shift in body size disparity \citep{alroy_fossil_1999}. 
Mesozoic mammals generally weighed less than 100 g and rarely over 5 kg \citep{benevento_early_2023}. 
The maximum body mass of mammals increased exponentially and by roughly three orders of magnitude by the late Eocene \citep{cooper_body_2010,smith_evolution_2010,raia_rapid_2013,slater_phylogenetic_2013}. 
It is thought that this increase is indicative of a wider expansion of niche breadth as mammals filled the vacant niches left by large dinosaurs.

\subsection{Early rise hypothesis}
While the suppression hypothesis is strongly supported by studies that have investigated changes in body size \citep{alroy_fossil_1999,slater_phylogenetic_2013}, body size is limited as a biodiversity metric for ecological variation since it can be influenced by several factors that may reflect stochastic rather than adaptive changes \citep{grossnickle_feeding_2020}. 
Recent studies instead focusing on morpho-functional traits (intrinsically linked to ecological function), such as jaw and dentition morphology, have instead suggested that eutherian diversity was attained through successive waves of diversification, starting far earlier than the K-Pg mass extinction \citep{grossnickle_untangling_2019}.
The \textit{early rise hypothesis} suggests an ecological diversification that occurred concurrently with the diversification of angiosperms and insects during and after the Cretaceous Terrestrial Revolution (125--80 Ma) (\autoref{fig:radiations}) \citep{meredith_impacts_2011,benton_angiosperm_2022}. 
For example, using morphometric analyses of molar shape \cite{grossnickle_therian_2016} found that eutherian disparity began increasing during the Late Cretaceous.
\cite{wilson_mammals_2013} also found a similar pattern, as well as a decrease in molar disparity across K-Pg suggesting selective extinction.
Ecological variation within Mesozoic mammals is not limited to diet; diversity in locomotor styles was reached as far back as the Jurassic in early eutherians \citep{chen_assembly_2019}. 
\subsection{Delayed rise hypothesis}
Other studies have pointed towards the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), an episode of elevated global temperatures 56 Ma that widened the latitudinal range of angiosperm-dominated forest, which in turn spurred the dispersal and radiation of eutherians (\autoref{fig:radiations}) \citep{bininda-emonds_delayed_2007}. 
\cite{benevento_patterns_2019} found that eutherian mammals had the most significant expansion in jaw morphospace at the onset of the Eocene.
\cite{bertrand_brawn_2022} investigated endocranial volume in Cenozoic placentals and also found a delayed radiation in diversity, highlighting increases in body size during the Paleocene preceded an expansion in brain size in the Eocene, which became necessary as competition intensified in an increasingly mammal-dominated biota. 
While these alternative hypotheses do not necessarily refute the influence of the K-Pg mass extinction, they instead suggest the extinction accentuated evolutionary trajectories already underway.

\section{Phylogenetic hypotheses} \label{phylogenies}
The origin and tempo of lineage diversification in placental mammals has been a point of contention for decades, meaning there is no undisputed phylogenetic topology for eutherian mammals.
Therefore, several studies investigating eutherian disparity have opted to time-bin the disparity data points by stratigraphy when assessing disparity through time \citep{grossnickle_mammal_2013,wilson_mammals_2013, grossnickle_therian_2016,benevento_patterns_2019}.
While this approach may be necessary in the absence of a well-resolved phylogeny, it inherently assumes a punctuated equilibrium model of morphological evolution. 
This assumption can be particularly problematic when assessing disparity across mass extinction events \citep{guillerme2018time}. 
The instantaneous nature of the K-Pg extinction requires a framework capable of capturing morphological changes at fine temporal scales. 
Stratigraphic time binning, which aggregates data over broad intervals, risks obscuring critical, transient shifts in disparity that may occur within shorter durations following the extinction. 
Incorporating a phylogeny means a time-slicing method can be used \citep{guillerme2018time}, which removes the punctuated assumption and can reveal more temporally refined patterns.

\subsection{Application of ancestral state estimation}
Ancestral state estimation is the broad method of estimating trait value(s) in a set of common ancestors given a set of observed data for tips on a phylogenetic tree \citep{schluter1997likelihood,cunningham1998reconstructing, yang2006computational,revell2025ancestral}.
Trait data may be continuous, such as body size, or discrete, such as diet type or presence/absence of a character. 
The application of ASE has been widely utilised in phylogenetic comparative methods, including disparity.


Intuitively, ancestral state estimation is appealing for those studying deep-time patterns of disparity.
Incorporating a phylogeny into disparity analyses will unavoidably introduce unsampled `ghost lineages' \citep{brusatte2011phylogenetic}.
A ghost lineage is the hypothetical ancestor that is not observed in the fossil record, but can be inferred to have existed due to the presence of other fossils/extant taxa. 
This can be best explained as the branch on a phylogeny between two observed taxonomic units (\autoref{fig:ichthy_phylo}).

Ancestral state estimation has been employed as a way of inferring the traits of these hypothetical species based on the traits of their descendents.
During ancestral state estimation, the estimated traits for nodes of a phylogeny are used as a way of sampling these ghost lineages.
This fills in the gaps in the fossil record and alleviates the bias associated with uneven sampling across the phylogenetic tree \citep{friedman2009ecomorphological, brusatte2011phylogenetic}.

\subsubsection{Likelihood methods}
Likelihood methods for ancestral state estimation are based on a unifying statistical framework that treats the internal node states as parameters to be estimated, seeking to maximize the probability of the observed tip states given a topology and a model of evolution \citep{joy2016ancestral}.
The central tenet of the approach is the likelihood function, which integrates information from the topology, which reflects the evolutionary history among taxa, and the model of trait evolution (which specifies how traits evolve along the branches). 
By systematically exploring the parameter space through numerical optimization, the method search for the ancestral states that maximize the likelihood function.
The same general likelihood framework underpins estimation of continuous and discrete characters; the core difference in estimation is the evolutionary model that is specified.

\subsubsection{Continuous characters} \label{continuous ace}
Continuous trait evolution is typically modelled under a Brownian motion (BM) model of evolution \citep{felsenstein1985phylogenies, omeara2006testing}.
BM models trait evolution as a directionless, stochastic process under which continuous characters undergo changes along the branches of a phylogeny.
These evolutionary changes come from a Gaussian distribution with a mean of 0 and variance proportional to the product of the rate of evolution and time elapsed ($\sigma^2\textit{t}$). 
Therefore, the expected amount of trait divergence between any two taxa is proportional to the amount of shared evolutionary history according to the phylogeny.

Under BM, the vector (\textbf{x}) of observed tip trait values follows a multivariate normal distribution (MVN) and has an expected value that is equal to the root state $x_0$.
\[
\boldsymbol{x} \sim \mathrm{MVN}(x_{0},\, \sigma^2 \boldsymbol{C})
\]

\textbf{\textit{C}} is a variance-covariance matrix for the shared ancestry of the taxa, defined by the tree height from the root to each tip (diagonal elements $\boldsymbol{C}_{ii}$) and the tree height above the root of the most recent common ancestor shared by each pair of tips (off-diagonal elements $\boldsymbol{C}_{ij}$).

When estimating ancestral states, the \textbf{\textit{C}} matrix is expanded to include the shared history of the internal nodes.
The evolutionary rate, $\sigma^2$, and root state $x_0$, are unknown parameters that can be estimated using Maximum Likelihood to give the multivariate normal distribution.
The trait values at internal nodes are then calculated as the conditional expectations within this distribution.
This means each node's estimated trait value is effectively a weighted average of the tip values, with the weights determined by the amount of shared evolutionary history (as defined by \textbf{\textit{C}}).


\subsubsection{Discrete characters} \label{ace discrete}
Ancestral state estimation of discrete characters are modelled using a continuous-time Markov chain process, known as the M\textit{k} model \citep{pagel1994detecting, yang2006computational}. 
This process is specified by a $\textit{k} \times \textit{k}$ matrix (\textit{k} being the number of possible states), \textbf{Q}, which gives the instantaneous transition rates (\textit{q}) between states for each character. 

\[
\textbf{Q} = \begin{bmatrix}
    -q_{0,1} & q_{0,1} \\
    q_{1,0} & -q_{1,0}
\end{bmatrix}
% =
% \begin{bmatrix}
%     -0.5 & 0.5 \\
%     0.5 & -0.5
% \end{bmatrix}
\]

Markov chain transition matrices have the necessary properties that all off-diagonal entries are non-negative, all diagonal properties are non-positive and the sum of each row is zero.
This is because in a continuous-time Markov process, probability flows out of a state (diagonal) and into other states (off-diagonal) and so the diagonal term represents the total rate of leaving a state.

The \textbf{Q} matrix is estimated using Maximum Likelihood; the value of \textbf{Q} that maximises the probability of the observed data given the tree topology. 
The matrix exponentiation of \textbf{Q} converts the instantaneous rates into probabilities of transitioning between states over a given time \textit{t}:
\[\textbf{P} = \exp(\textbf{Q}\textit{t})\]

Global marginal estimation, which is most commonly implemented in software, fixes the value of \textbf{Q} and calculates the probability of each state at each node, given the observed data at the tips.
These likelihoods are rescaled by their sum so that they add up to 1.0, and so represent an empirical Bayes posterior probability \citep{yang2006computational,revell2025ancestral}. 
Since this can become computationally intensive as the phylogeny increases in size, in practice functions use algorithms such as Felsenstein's pruning algorithm \citep{felsenstein1981evolutionary}.

\subsubsection{Uncertainty in estimates}
A critical issue within ancestral state estimation is that estimates tend to have broad uncertainty \citep{schluter1997likelihood}.
This uncertainty is not uniform across the tree: it typically increases towards root of the tree, where direct information from observed tip data becomes increasingly diffuse \citep{gascuel2014predicting}.
For discrete characters, this uncertainty manifests as inconclusive scaled likelihoods, i.e., state 0 = 0.51, state 1 = 0.49.
For continuous characters, uncertainty is wide confidence intervals.

How this uncertainty in estimations should be dealt with is equivocal.
Despite the encouragement for uncertainty to be carried forward in comparative analyses \citep{silvestro2015measurement}, most computational implementations of ASE return point estimates.
For continuous characters, this will be the median value of the confidence interval and for discrete characters, it will be the most probable state according to a scaled likelihood threshold.
By ignoring uncertainty, point estimates can lead to overconfidence in results and erroneous inferences in downstream analyses (such as down the disparity pipeline), all while obscuring potentially `accurate' uncertainty \citep{ackerly2006niche,vanderpoorten2006mapping}.

For discrete characters, there is a level of subjectivity in how to collapse the states into the most probable based on their scaled likelihood.
It is common to have a threshold implementation in ancestral state estimation software, whereby the user can set a scaled likelihood at which point states are collapsed to a point estimate, or left as uncertain, which is denoted as `0/1', for example.
However, these thresholds are often arbitrarily set with no guidance of what is the `correct' value \citep{smith2022evolution,casali2023morphological}.
Much of the subjectivity relates to the trade-off between permissiveness and conservativeness; higher thresholds are likely too conservative and so too many states are left uncertain, perhaps missing true signals.
Whereas lower thresholds may be too permissive and can lead to erroneous inferences.
Interestingly, \cite{keating2023best} highlights that uncertainty returned by a specified model is a better predictor of accuracy than likelihood diagnostics, such as AIC.

\subsubsection{Sensitivity to fossil sampling}
Given that uncertainty is often high in ancestral state estimation, how then can estimates be improved?
\cite{ane2008analysis} demonstrates that ancestral state estimation is \textit{statistically inconsistent}, meaning that even if the sample size of extant tips is infinite, estimates are not expected to converge on the true value.
Instead, the inclusion of fossils can improve estimates, due to the shorter evolutionary path to the root of the tree \citep{finarelli2006ancestral,finarelli2013potential}.
Fossils can be used as priors on nodes, or simply added to the tree as tips.

By extension, inclusion of fossils can greatly influence inferences into disparity.
Extant-only datasets often miss early rapid morphological diversification signals \citep{harmon2010early}, whereas analyses incorporating fossil data often finds the reverse \citep{hughes2013clades}
For example,~\cite{mitchell2015extant} found that analyses of bird disparity were underestimated when using ancestral state estimation of extant-only datasets.

\subsubsection{Model dependency}
Another key challenge is the sensitivity to violations of model assumptions.
For continuous characters, this is particularly pertinent when traits have evolved under non-BM \citep{revell2025ancestral}.
When trait evolution deviates from BM -- due to directional trends, bounded evolution, or regime shifts -- ancestral state estimation can return biased estimations of ancestral states.

Both \cite{webster2002testing} and \cite{finarelli2006ancestral} were unable to recover accurate ancestral estimates of body size in planktonic foraminifera and mammals, respectively, due to a trend of increase through time.
Trends mean a moving average through time, which violates the assumption of zero-mean change under BM.
Similarly, issues estimating the evolutionary rate may arise when the trait data evolved under an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) model, whereby trait data evolves under a stabilizing selection \citep{royer2016comparison}.
It is common for regime shifts to occur at mass extinction boundaries \citep{jablonski1986background}.
For example,~\cite{slater_phylogenetic_2013} found that mammal body size was relatively constrained (OU) up until the K-Pg mass extinction which then allowed a release of constraint and BM evolution.
Such shifts in regime could affect accurate estimation of evolutionary rate and thus state estimates.

As discussed previously, fossil sampling is crucial for accurate estimation, especially when model assumptions are violated \citep{puttick2016partially}.
Fossils assist in estimating and constraining model parameters, as well as distinguishing different evolutionary processes \citep{goswami2025phenomics}. 
For example, when estimating states under an OU model, fossils are necessary for estimating the primary optimum.
Similarly, fossils would be necessary to detect a trend, such as Cope's rule.
A similar logic applies to discrete characters; \cite{keating2023best} found that inclusion of fossils improved estimate accuracy, irrespective of model misspecification.

\section{Statistical tests in disparity}
Given a calculation of disparity, it is necessary for this value to be statistically tested in the context of the research question.
A popular choice is to carry out a PERMANOVA, which can be used to assess whether two groups share the same centroid and dispersion in the morphospace \citep{moon2020early,apaldetti2021sauropodomorph,donati2025continuity}.
Alternatively, studies may opt to investigate change in disparity, for example through the use of a t test \citep{patton2021adaptive,haug2023quantitative,wang2023low}.
Therefore, a t-test may be preferred if investigating a nuanced change in morphospace that PERMANOVA cannot capture, such as change in variance.


The way these tests are implemented depends on the research question, but questions tend to relate to differences between groups (between clades for example) or through time (across time bins).
However, using a single `average change' statistic to compare disparity through time has limitations, notably that it ignores directional trends.
Discounting trends in evolution may implicitly assume a punctuated model of evolution, where abrupt phenotypic shifts are interpreted as adaptive but are actually a reflection of stochastic processes \citep{hunt2015simple,pagel2022general}.
This punctuated bias is amplified when time binning disparity across stratigraphic bins.
Despite its popularity, this method is flawed in that it only allows interpretation of changes in disparity between bins, rather than within.
By aggregating data over broad intervals, the lack of resolution risks obscuring critical, transient shifts in disparity that may occur within shorter durations (following an extinction for example). 
This issue can be alleviated through time-slicing, whereby intervals are not punctuated by time bins but sub-sampling at equidistant points across a phylogeny \citep{guillerme2018time}.

There have been several methods already developed for identifying non-random shifts in disparity.
A common approach is to implement a null model to see if the empirical data deviates \citep{freckleton2006detecting, murrell2018global}. 
For example, the global rank envelope test implemented by \cite{murrell2018global} identifies non-random shifts as when the empirical Disparity-Through-Time curve (see \cite{harmon2003tempo}) falls within the most extreme 5\% ranked curves of a null Brownian model.
Similarly, \cite{smith2022evolution} made use of simulations of discrete trait data as a null model based on the zero-force evolutionary law (the tendency for diversity to increase through time) \citep{mcshea2010biology}, to identify points at which disparity is greater/less than expected.
However, a framework is needed that allows a statistical test to be applied over large numbers of time series, when inspecting individual time series becomes impractical.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Simulations}
Simulations were carried out using \texttt{treats} \citep{guillerme2024treats}.
Trees were simulated using a modified version of the birth-death process algorithm.
The process begins with a single lineage and iteratively selects lineages to grow the tree forward in time. 
At each step, all living branches are extended by a branch length increment, drawn from an exponential distribution based on the total number of lineages and the combined speciation and extinction rates. 
Selected lineages then undergo either speciation or extinction based on a probabilistic decision determined by the specified rates.
100 trees were simulated with birth-death parameters as speciation, $\lambda = 1.0$, and extinction, $\mu = 0.7$.
Trees were fixed to have 50 extant tips and a range of 50 - 200 fossil tips.

Across each tree, discrete and continuous trait data were simulated.
Discrete trait data were simulated using three different Markov chain matrices, representing fast (0.9), medium (0.1) and slow (0.01) transition rates, for 100 characters (85\% binary and 15\% three-state).
Continuous trait data were simulated under Brownian motion (BM) and Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) models, with default parameters and for 50 characters.
Trait data were simulated using the function \texttt{map.traits}, which assigns both nodes and tips trait values, essentially constructing full hypothetical evolutionary histories across each phylogeny for each trait type.

In order to simulate different levels of fossil sampling, each tree was split into four chronological `stages' to replicate the stratigraphic nature of the fossil record.
From each `stage', fossil tips were randomly pruned from the tree and corresponding trait matrix at five levels:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Living: only extant tips are retained
    \item Low: 5\% fossils retained
    \item Medium: 15\% fossils retained
    \item High: 50\% fossils retained
    \item All: All fossils retained
\end{itemize}
I aim to modify this method in the future so that earlier stages have lower fossil sampling than more recent stages, to replicate the temporal gradient in completeness seen in the empirical fossil record \citep{davies2017completeness}.

\subsection{Ancestral state estimation}
Node states were then removed from the trait matrices leaving just tip states.
Ancestral states were estimated using the \texttt{multi.ace} function in the \texttt{dispRity} package \citep{guillerme2018disprity}.
\subsubsection{Discrete characters}
For discrete state estimation, \texttt{multi.ace} is a wrapper for \texttt{castor::asr\_mk\_model}.  
Ancestral states were estimated using marginal likelihood estimation, whereby each state at each node is assigned a scaled likelihood (\autoref{ace discrete}).
Symmetrical transition rates were set \textit{a priori} during simulations and so the SYM model was used to avoid model misspecification as a confounding factor on estimation accuracy \citep{keating2023best}.
Three different majority-rule approaches were implemented for collapsing the state probabilities into ancestral states.
The \textit{strict majority rule} uses a threshold that selects the state with a scaled likelihood equal or greater than a designated value (in this case 0.5).
The \textit{relative majority rule} sets a threshold at a scaled likelihood greater than the maximum observed scaled likelihood minus the inverse number of possible states, \textit{k}
\[
\textit{Relative majority threshold} = \max(L_{\text{scaled}}) - \frac{1}{k}
\]
The \textit{no majority rule} (sampling approach) returns multiple matrices (\textit{x} matrices) where ancestral states are probabilistically sampled based on their scaled likelihoods. 
For each node, the state assigned in each matrix is randomly drawn according to the probability distribution of the scaled likelihoods, meaning states with higher likelihoods are more likely to be selected across the sample of matrices. 
For example, a node with uncertain estimates (e.g., state 0 = 0.6, state 1 = 0.4) will be assigned state 0 in approximately 60\% of the matrices and state 1 in 40\% of the matrices.
In this case, 100 matrices were returned using the no majority rule.

\subsubsection{Continuous characters}
Continuous ancestral states were estimated using the BM likelihood method described in \autoref{continuous ace}.
Two approaches were applied to account for uncertainty in estimates.
The \textit{point estimate} approach simply returns the median of the 95\% confidence interval.
Alternatively, the \textit{sampling} approach, whereby trait values are sampled across a normal distribution defined by the 95\% confidence interval at each node, over a specified number of matrices (in this case 100).

A Hamming distance metric to measure the raw accuracy at each node and state between the true and estimated discrete matrices.
Both uncertain (where no state surpasses the threshold set by the majority rules) and inaccurate estimates were penalised with a score of 1, thus only correct estimates were not penalised (score of 0).
To achieve fair comparison between each fossil sampling level, only the nodes present across all fossil sampling levels were included in these analyses (leaving 50 nodes).
This outputted a distance matrix of dimensions \textit{N} by \textit{C}, whereby \textit{N} is the number of nodes and \textit{C} is the number of characters.
The mean was taken from each matrix to output a distance score between 0--1.

For continuous characters, estimation error was quantified by calculating the Euclidean distance between the estimated and true trait values at each node. 
Trait values were Z-score standardised prior to analysis to ensure fair comparison between BM and OU models.


\subsection{Measuring error in disparity}
To evaluate how ancestral state estimation influences disparity estimates, both the true and estimated trait matrices were analysed using a standard disparity pipeline (\autoref{fig:pipeline}).
For discrete characters, the raw trait matrices were converted into distance matrices using the MORD metric \citep{lloyd_estimating_2016}, the most widely used distance metric in disparity analyses.
These distance matrices were ordinated using principal coordinates analysis (PCO) to generate the multidimensional traitspaces.
Disparity was quantified as the sum of variances across all PCO axes for each combination of tree, transition rate, fossil sampling level, and majority-rule method.
To measure the estimation accuracy in disparity, the estimated disparity value was subtracted by the true disparity value and then divided by the true disparity value, to give a relative difference in disparity (this is necessary since true disparity values can vary greatly between different parameter combinations).

For continuous characters, the trait matrices were ordinated using principal component analysis (PCA) and then also disparity measured using the sum of variances across all PC axes.
These analyses were performed using the \texttt{dispRity} package \citep{guillerme2018disprity}.

\section{Results and Discussion}
\subsection{Discrete characters}
% \subsubsection{Raw distance}

Raw ancestral state estimation error across all nodes varies with the majority-rule method, transition rate and fossil sampling level (\autoref{fig:rawdistance}).
Across all majority rules, transition rates strongly influenced estimation accuracy.
Estimation accuracy was consistently highest when transition rates were slow.
Medium and fast rates resulted in substantially higher error, particularly under strict and relative majority rules.
The threshold-based strict and relative majority rule methods were less accurate in estimation compared to the sample-based approach, particularly with medium and fast transition rates.
Notably, fossil sampling level had a relatively negligible effect on accuracy, which contrasts previous findings \citep{finarelli2006ancestral,keating2023best}.
Higher sampling slightly improved estimates under strict and relative majority rules at fast and medium rates.
In contrast, the sample-based approach remained stable across all sampling levels, highlighting its robustness.

\subsection{Continuous characters}
Raw estimation accuracy for continuous characters was found to vary substantially between models of trait evolution and method of handling uncertainty, but not across fossil sampling levels (\autoref{fig:contdist}).
Estimation accuracy of OU traits were more accurate than accuracy of BM trait estimation.
This is intuitive, since estimation accuracy is expected to decrease as the trait variance, $\sigma$, increases \citep{royer2016comparison}.
BM represents unbounded trait evolution and the variance
In an OU model, trait variance is restricted due to the stabilising selection, denoted by the $\alpha$ parameter. 
Therefore, it can be inferred that the greater the strength of $\alpha$, the more accurate the estimations (something that will be tested in future analyses).

Interestingly, fossil sampling appears to make no difference on the estimation accuracy, nor the precision.
