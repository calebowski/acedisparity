\begin{abstract} %NC: I won't comment on this now as I assume it will change as we progress.
\noindent
Ancestral state estimation is widely used to infer unobserved traits across a phylogeny, extending analyses beyond incomplete fossil records. %NC: I think this might need a bit of rearrangement to focus more on disparity and ase instead of starting out as if you're going to outline ase in general
In studies of morphological disparity ancestral state estimation can account for ghost lineages, yet estimates are often confounded with high uncertainty
How best to deal with this uncertainty remains unclear. 
Using simulations, we compared pre- and post-ordination estimation methods, and both point estimate and probabilistic approaches for treating uncertainty, under continuous and discrete trait models and varying fossil sampling levels.
The relative disparity error was calculated from the difference between our estimated disparity values and the true disparity values from the original simulations.
We then extended this framework to evaluate the recovery of changes in disparity across time by simulating mass extinction events with varying intensity and selectivity.
We find that pre-ordination probabilistic estimations outperform post-ordination and point estimation methods and that omission of ancestral state estimations leads to underestimates of disparity.
As expected, higher fossil sampling improves estimation accuracy, although recovered disparity accuracy was robust even at low fossil sampling. 
Similar patterns were observed when recovering changes in disparity across mass extinction events but declined significantly when fossil sampling was low, highlighting that fossil inclusion is essential when assessing disparity in deep time.
These results demonstrate that explicitly propagating uncertainty, rather than collapsing states into point estimates, is more likely to recover accurate patterns in disparity, reinforcing the need to move beyond point estimates when working with the incomplete fossil record.
\end{abstract}

Morphological diversity, hereafter termed disparity, is widely used for investigating macroevolutionary dynamics \citep{guillerme2020disparities}. % aware that need more ref's here. 
Disparity can be described as the distribution or spread of taxa within a multidimensional trait space defined by morphological similarity \citep{erwin_disparity_2007} and can be quantified using both continuous \citep{garcia2017phenotypic} and discrete characters \citep{foote1994morphological, lloyd2016estimating}.
Morphological trait datasets are routinely integrated with phylogenies when examining disparity across clades or time.
These analyses can be used to investigate selectivity across mass extinctions \citep{bapst2012graptoloid,cole2021selectivity, liu2024heterogeneous}, can identify timings of key morphological innovations \citep{foth2021rapid}. % and something else...
However, such analyses are inevitably limited by the incompleteness of the fossil record; only a biased subset of past diversity is preserved, leaving unsampled ghost lineages and obscuring large portions of a clade's evolutionary history \citep{flannery2019does,smith2021phylogenetic}.
This missing data has been shown to result in both under- and over-estimations of disparity \citep{mitchell2015extant,smith2023fossilization}.
For example, high amounts of missing data results in `centroid-slippage', where an incomplete taxon is pulled towards the centre of the trait space \citep{flannery2019does}.
Conversely, exceptional preservation, such as Lagerstätten taxa may result in inflated disparity, seen in ichthyosaurs \citep{flannery2019does} and pterosaurs \citep{butler2012geological}.

\subsection{Issues with ASE}
Despite its theoretical advantages, %TG: it's not clear from the reading what advantages you mean here
 integration of \ase within the disparity analysis pipeline has often been neglected.
Much of the apprehension towards the use of \ase has likely stemmed from the notion that estimates are inherently uncertain; root-ward estimates in particular are often associated with large confidence intervals or inconclusive scaled likelihoods \citep{schluter1997likelihood, losos1999uncertainty,gascuel2014predicting}.
Despite the encouragement for uncertainty to be carried forward in comparative analyses \citep{silvestro2015measurement,boyko2024dentist}, most computational implementations of \ase return point estimates, since there is little guidance on how to correctly treat this uncertainty downstream when constructing the trait space. 
These point estimates are typically averages from confidence intervals for continuous characters or collapsed states returned from arbitrary thresholds \citep{smith2022evolution,casali2023morphological}. % see casali, tj smith disparity fungal paper. %TG: again, good to add that as an exanmple!
By ignoring uncertainty, point estimates can lead to overconfidence in results and erroneous inferences in downstream analyses, all while obscuring potentially accurate signals \citep{ackerly2006niche,vanderpoorten2006mapping}.

Another key issue with \ase is its sensitivity to model misspecification.
Likelihood-based approaches to \ase require both a tree topology and a specified model of character evolution.
Estimation of continuous characters are typically modeled under Brownian motion (BM), which describes a directionless, stochastic process where the trait divergence between related taxa is proportional to their shared evolutionary history and the rate of evolution \citep{felsenstein1985phylogenies,schluter1997likelihood,omeara2006testing}.
However, deviations from BM %TG: no acronym here would be easier to read as well
 in the trait data, such as directional trends or stabilising selection, can result in inaccurate estimations. 
For example, both \cite{webster2002testing} and \cite{finarelli2006ancestral} were unable to recover accurate ancestral estimates of body size in planktonic foraminifera and mammals, respectively, due to a trend of increase through time.
Model-related issues can also arise for discrete traits, which are estimated using an M\textit{k} model, requiring a tree topology and specified model of evolution (describing transition rates).
Estimation accuracy of discrete states appears prone to model over-parameterisation and sensitive to fast transition rates \citep{keating2023best}.
Furthermore discrete \ase faces a paradoxical trade-off where a slow rate of evolution allows for an easy estimation of ancestral states, but provides little information for estimating transition rates \citep{gascuel2020darwinian}.

\subsection{Previous work}
Numerous studies have assessed the raw estimation error of \ase approaches under different models of evolution, methods of estimation and fossil sampling level \citep{elliot2014inferring,royer2016comparison, reyes2018testing, holland2020accuracy,keating2023best,boyko2025uncertainty}. 
However, how state estimation accuracy is propagated into recovery of an accurate trait space is less understood, despite the continued use of \ase in disparity analyses.
\cite{lloyd2018journeys} demonstrated that carrying out \ase post-ordination rather than pre-ordination can distort the distribution of points in a trait space markedly, introducing unwanted phylogenetic signal and low levels of convergence.
However, this study was restricted to a single empirical dataset and to discrete traits.
Thus, we lack a comprehensive understanding of how estimation approaches, trait data and fossil sampling level interact to affect our ability to recover accurate patterns in trait space.

\subsection{Aims}
Using a simulation framework, we expand on this work to evaluate the ability of different \ase approaches to recover true disparity values across different evolutionary models and fossil sampling regimes.
Simulations offer a robust approach for benchmarking methodologies since a ground-truth dataset can be generated, so trait values and the resulting disparity signal are all known \textit{a priori} \citep{lotterhos2022simulation}.
Different methodological choices and estimation scenarios can then be evaluated against the true disparity value. 
Specifically, we directly compare pre- versus post-ordination estimation approaches, as well as point-estimate versus probabilistic treatments of uncertainty, thereby examining two axes of methodological variation.
We also investigate how estimation error varies under different models of trait evolution and fossil sampling regimes.
This integrative framework provides a comprehensive evaluation for understanding when \ase improves disparity estimation, when it introduces bias, and how uncertainty should be treated when working with incomplete fossil data.

\subsubsection{Trait simulations} \label{trait simulation}
For each phylogeny, we simulated continuous and discrete trait data separately. %NC: I swapped d and c around as you talk about cont data first.
Continuous trait data were simulated under variations of Brownian motion (BM) and Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) models (table \ref{tab:models}). 
We simulated BM with variance = 0.25.
We also simulated BM with a trend ($BM_t$), where the ancestral trait value increased by an increment of 0.1 multiplied by branch length.
For OU models, the data were simulated with strong attraction to the optimum ($\alpha$) %NC: strong what? pull towards the optimum? 
($OU_{st}$), weak $\alpha$ ($OU_w$) and with differing optimum and root states ($OU_{sh}$).
Following \cite{gearty2024impact}, the $\alpha$ parameter was set based on phylogenetic half life ($\frac{\mathit{ln(2)}} {\alpha}$) to account for differing tree heights across simulations \citep{cooper2016cautionary}.
For the strong and weak OU models, $\alpha$ was set as 25\% and 75\% of the tree height respectively.
We simulated 20 independent characters for each model (\textit{BM}, $BM_t$, $OU_w$, $OU_{st}$, $OU_{sh}$) across each phylogeny.

Discrete trait evolution was simulated using a discrete-time Markov process, where the probability of state transition along each branch was proportional to the product of the transition probability and branch length.
We simulated discrete traits across three rate categories, representing fast (0.9), medium (0.1) and slow (0.01) transition rates (table \ref{tab:models}). 
These rates were assigned by generating equal-rates (ER) transition probability matrices.
For each category, 85 binary characters and 15 three-state characters were simulated, following a typical scoring ratio found in empirical data \citep{guillerme2016effects, casali2023morphological}.

\subsubsection{Uncertainty treatment} \label{uncertainty}
For each estimation method, we also compared point estimate and probabilistic approaches. 
The point estimate approach returns a fixed single value for each estimate.
For continuous characters, the point estimate returned was the median of the ancestral estimate confidence interval. 
For discrete characters, we used a strict majority rule where the state with the highest scaled likelihood was selected at each node for each character.
In scenarios where state likelihoods were equal, one state was chosen at random.
We also used a relative majority rule, where any state at a scaled likelihood greater than the maximum observed scaled likelihood minus the inverse number of possible states was collapsed, otherwise states were left as uncertain (supplementary).

The probabilistic approach differs in that uncertainty in estimates are explicitly accounted for by sampling multiple results rather than a single point estimate. 
For continuous characters, we sampled each state across 100 matrices, with each estimate value lying within a normal distribution centered on the mean ancestral estimation and spread across the estimated confidence interval.
Each discrete character state at each node was sampled 100 times according to their scaled likelihoods (resulting in 100 character estimation matrices). 
In a scenario where there is uncertainty in a binary character for a specific node, e.g., 0.55 for state 0 and 0.45 for state 1, this distribution approach results in a 55\% probability of sampling state 0 in each matrix for the respective node (and 45\% chance of sampling state 1).
Both procedures were implemented using the \texttt{sample = 100} argument in the \texttt{dispRity::multi.ace} function \citep{guillerme2018disprity}.

\subsection{Disparity analyses}
When assessing overall disparity, we wanted to understand how well ancestral state estimation can recover true patterns in size and density in trait space occupancy \citep{guillerme2020shifting}.

Size-based metrics describe the spread of points in the trait space \citep{guillerme2020shifting}.
To explore how well the size of the trait space was recovered, we calculated the sum of quantiles.
The sum of quantiles calculates the range on each PC axis for the 95\% of the data and then sums these ranges across all axes.
This metric has similar properties to the often used sum of ranges metric by describing the total trait space exploited \citep{foote1992rarefaction}, but with less sensitivity to outliers and sample size.
For density, we used the sum of variances \citep{ciampaglio2001detecting,wills2001morphological} and the average nearest neighbour distance \citep{foote1992rarefaction}. 
% The sum of variances is a very widely used metric in disparity analyses. %TG: pro intuition writing tip: if you say "the most used" I'd probably want to see a meta analysis to prove it. if you say "very widely used" I can agree with you without any extra reference/work ;)
Disparity values were calculated across each ancestral state estimation and the true simulated values so that a relative disparity error could be calculated (Fig. \ref{fig:pipeline} Step 5).

\begin{equation}
\text{Relative disparity error} = \frac{\text{Estimated disparity} - \text{True disparity}}{\text{True disparity}}
\end{equation}

We calculated disparity across three different treatments of the ordinated matrices.
First, we used the untreated ordination output to examine how well ancestral state estimation recovers the true disparity signal across all PC axes. 
Second, we min-max standardised the PC axes so that the absolute magnitude in PC scores did not influence the disparity metric scores (i.e. in this case, the max-score for PC1 was always standardised to 1).
Third, we only used the minimum number of dimensions that were contained across all fossil sampling levels for that specific phylogeny so that each fossil sampling level, i.e., the first 48, 98 and 148 dimensions for the 50, 100 and 150 living taxa simulations, respectively.
This was to negate any effect that higher-dimensional trait space estimations were more likely to be closer the true disparity signal.

\subsection{Statistical tests}
The relative disparity error values were converted to absolute errors to measure the magnitude of deviation from the true disparity, regardless of direction.
Due to the zero-inflated and right-skewed nature of the error distribution, we applied a log-transformation ($log(|x|+0.001)$).
Since these errors were now log-errors, essentially the more negative the log error the more accurate (closer to 0) the original relative error is.
Given the imbalance in sample sizes between the different methods, i.e., distribution approaches return 100 disparity values for every 1 point estimate, we aggregated the distribution outputs by taking the median prior to analyses.
We also performed subsequent analyses where the distribution was retained, but each individual point returned by distributions was weighted 1/100th of the single point estimate.


We investigated the drivers of disparity estimation accuracy with linear-mixed models (LMM) using the \texttt{lme4} R package \citep{bates2015fitting}.
The log-transformed relative error was treated as the response variable. 
Based on our experimental design, we fitted a four-way interaction between the estimation method, model of evolution, fossil sampling level and disparity metric.
We also included tree size as a further non-interactive fixed effect.
To account for the variation with each simulation run, each tree replicate was included as a random intercept.
We carried out post-hoc pairwise comparisons using estimated marginal means, adjusted using Tukey's HSD.

\subsection{Continuous traits}
\subsubsection{Methods of estimation}
For continuous characters, the method of estimation had a significant effect on disparity accuracy ($F = 358,~ p < .001$) (\autoref{fig:continuous boxplot}, \autoref{tab: anova continuous lmm}).
Notably, using ancestral state estimation methods were found to improve estimates of disparity compared to excluding ancestral states when averaged over all scenarios (\autoref{tab:continuous method cld}).
Post-hoc pairwise comparisons of estimated marginal means (\textit{emmeans}) revealed both pre- and post-ordination distributions of ancestral state estimations returned the lowest log disparity errors. 
In contrast, point estimates performed worse overall than bypassing ancestral state estimation (\autoref{tab:continuous method cld}).

\subsubsection{Interaction effects}
However, this pattern was context-specific.
Fossil sampling was the strongest driver of recovery of an accurate disparity signal for continuous traits ($F = 9169,~ p < .001$), as each increase in fossil sampling level resulted in a significant reduction in disparity error.
Similarly, the evolutionary model ($F = 2956, p < .001$) and disparity metric ($F = 5028, p < .001$) were also strong drivers of variation in error.
Tree size had a minor effect in comparison ($F = 43, p < .001$).
The three-way interaction between estimation method, fossil sampling and evolutionary model was significant ($F = 60, p < .001$) and the post-hoc estimated marginal means can be used to identify the optimal method for models at each fossil sampling levels.
For Brownian motion (BM) at 100\% fossil sampling, the distribution estimations were most accurate. 
At 50\% and below, methods of \ase generally performed equally well and significantly better than excluding ancestral states. 
Under BM + trend, a similar pattern was followed at high fossil sampling levels, yet when fossil sampling drops below 15\% methods of \ase perform worse than excluding ancestral states. 
In contrast, under an OU model excluding ancestral states was the best approach across all fossil sampling levels.
While the disparity metric had a large effect on the overall estimation accuracy ($F = 5028,~ p < .001$), it was a relatively lower driver of variation regarding its interaction with the estimation method ($F = 7, p < .001$).
Therefore, while estimation accuracy differed between metrics, the hierarchy between optimal methods was generally consistent across metrics.

\textbf{Key takeaways:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Use \textbf{pre/post ord distribution} estimations when trait data is BM (+ trend).
    \item If trait data has a trend, fossil sampling >5\% is essential to return reliable disparity signal.
    \item When trait data is OU, it is most accurate to exclude ancestral states at all fossil sampling levels.
\end{itemize}



\subsection{Discrete traits}
\subsubsection{Methods of estimation} 
For discrete characters, the method of estimation also had a significant effect on disparity accuracy ($F = 10767, p < .001$) (\autoref{fig:discrete raw boxplot}).
Similar to continuous traits, methods of ancestral state estimation consistently improved disparity estimates compared to excluding ancestral states (\autoref{tab:discrete method cld}). 
However, the optimal type of estimation differed. 
Post-hoc pairwise comparisons of estimated marginal means revealed that pre-ordination point estimates returned the lowest log disparity errors overall. 
In contrast to continuous traits, distribution methods generally performed worse than pre-ordination point estimates on average, but post-ordination point estimates performed worse than excluding ancestral states (\autoref{tab:discrete method cld}).
\subsubsection{Interaction effects} 
Similar to continuous traits, fossil sampling ($F = 22191,~ p < .001$), transition rate ($F = 6957, p < .001$) and metric ($F = 26174, p < .001$) were again major drivers of variation in accuracy.
As expected, increased fossil sampling and decreased transition rate led to lower disparity errors, and the mean pairwise distance was the easiest metric to recover, whereas the sum of quantiles was the hardest.
The three-way interaction between estimation method, fossil sampling and transition rate explained much of the variation in the data ($F = 588, p < .001$).
The broad pattern was that at high fossil sampling levels, pre-ordination point estimates were the best method, whereas at lower fossil sampling levels both pre- and post-ordination distributions returned the most accurate disparity values.
This pattern was model-dependent, however, as under a slow rate the pre-ordination distribution method was the best performing method across all fossil sampling levels.
At fast and medium rates under low fossil sampling (< 15\%), the post-ordination distribution method returned the lowest disparity errors.
Notably, methods of ancestral state estimation outperformed their exclusion across all combinations of transition rate, fossil sampling level and disparity metric.
Again, while the disparity metric was a significant driver to overall accuracy, it had little effect on the hierarchy in methods set by the transition rate and fossil sampling level, signified by a weak four-way interaction ($F = 10, p < .001$.)

\textbf{Key takeaways:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Use \textbf{pre-ordination point} estimations when fossil sampling is dense >15\% and transition rates are medium/fast
    \item Use \textbf{post-ordination distribution} when fossil sampling is low <15\% and transition rates are medium/fast
    \item If rates are slow, the \textbf{pre-ordination distribution} method is best across all fossil sampling levels
    \item It is encouraged to always use \ase, since it improve estimations across all scenarios.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}
Our results suggest that, overall, the inclusion of ancestral state can improve our estimations of disparity compared to their exclusion.
However, we found this pattern to be context-specific, particularly dependent on fossil sampling and model of evolution.
Overall, we can recommend that ancestral state estimations be always be used in estimations of disparity from discrete characters, but recommend caution for continuous characters.

% \subsection{Continuous characters}
\subsection{Pre versus post ord}
For continuous characters, we found virtually no discrepancy between the effect of pre- and post-ordination estimation of ancestral states on disparity.
Given that continuous traits exist within a Euclidean space, the linear transformations of both PCA and \ase under Brownian motion (BM) mean that estimated node states exist within the same relative positions to the observed tip data for both pre- and post-ordination estimation.

For discrete traits, the timing of \ase was a key driver in disparity accuracy.
Overall, pre-ordination estimation led to more accurate disparity estimations than post-ordination estimation, a finding consistent with \cite{lloyd2018journeys}.
Post-ordination estimation is ultimately limited by its geometric assumptions.
Since axes of the trait space are treated as continuous traits evolving under BM, the ancestral nodes are inevitably constrained within the convex hull defined by the observed tip data \citep{stayton2015definition,lloyd2018journeys,gerber2019use}.
This forces the implicit assumption that our observed trait data accounts for the extremes of morphological diversity for the clade of interest and that past diversity was only a subset of modern diversity.
Disparity is therefore underestimated, particularly with range-based metrics such as sum of quantiles (\autoref{fig:discrete raw boxplot}).
Estimation of discrete ancestral states prior to ordination does not encounter these same limitations.
A discrete trait matrix can be said to exist within a hypercube, where each vertex corresponds to a unique combination of traits \citep{gerber2019use}.
When the ancestral states  are estimated prior to ordination, there is no constraint preventing an ancestor from occupying a vertex that lies peripheral to the observed data.
Previously authors have interpreted estimated ancestral nodes that fall outside the observed range (convex hull) as an artificial inflation of disparity \citep{brusatte2011phylogenetic}.
However, as \cite{gerber2019use} clarifies, this interpretation is flawed, since the convex hull is a two-dimensional projection of the true trait space.
As exhibited in \autoref{fig:discrete raw boxplot}, underestimation of disparity is ubiquitous to all discrete character estimation methods. 
% Therefore, an optimal method is that which does not constrain ancestral nodes, which is the pre-ordination method.
Therefore, pre-ordination discrete estimations are not bound to a cloud of averages in the way post-ordination estimation is and thus are optimal at recovering true trait space expanse.



\subsection{Treatment of uncertainty}
The choice of estimation method for continuous characters was largely driven by the treatment of uncertainty.
At high fossil sampling levels, incorporating uncertainty through the use of distributions performed better than point estimates (\autoref{fig:continuous boxplot}; \autoref{fig:continuous heatmap}).
This advantage stems from the tendency of point estimates to smooth over evolutionary history \citep{boyko2024dentist,boyko2025uncertainty}, underestimating the variance of the true evolutionary path.
However, in contrast to discrete traits, continuous estimation distributions are unbounded and thus sensitive to data quality.
Therefore, the variation introduced from sampling across a distribution was dependent on the density of fossils; at high fossil density, confidence intervals are constrained due to higher certainty in estimates.
As fossil sampling decreases, these confidence intervals are widened and more noise is introduced as a result of using distribution, negating the advantage observed at higher fossil sampling.

For discrete characters, we found an inverse pattern where distribution methods were optimal at low fossil sampling levels and point estimates were optimal at high fossil sampling levels.
This is due to the bounded nature of discrete traits; even with minimal information, the disparity error that can be returned is bounded by the number of states present at each node, which contrasts the effectively infinite error that can be returned by continuous characters.
In terms of the pre-ordination estimations, our results reveal two issues relating to uncertainty in estimations.
At low fossil sampling or slow transition rates, the predominant issue facing the recovery of a true trait space is the lack of data.
For fossil sampling, the lack of evidence in deeper time means our ancestral state estimates, and subsequent trait space pattern, are biased by the living tip data. 
In the case of slow transition rates, we face a paradoxical situation with slow rates where the root state can be easily estimated, yet the few transitions make the rate of change hard to estimate\cite{gascuel2020darwinian}.
If we use the hypercube analogy again, the state scaled likelihoods at each node under slow rates or low/absent fossils will be biased against transitions and when point estimates are used ancestral nodes will be constrained within a certain region of vertices in the trait space.
In contrast, pre-ordination distribution estimations allow the exploration of other vertices by sampling across alternative discrete state combinations.
In the case of slow rates and low fossil sampling, this was found to improve disparity estimations.
The opposite side of the spectrum of discrete state `uncertainty' is the issue of saturated data, where the paradox is flipped and instead, we have an abundance of information, thus transition rates are easy to estimate, yet due to the rate of change most nodes are left with inconclusive scaled likelihoods.
Under the fast rates simulated in this study, character transitions are saturated, so there is very little phylogenetic signal.
The use of distributions amplifies the noise in this scenario, thus point estimates can be used to filter out the noise. 

\subsection{Model misspecification \& Fossil inclusion}
As expected, we found that the differences in disparity errors between models of evolution reflect model misspecification in ancestral state estimation. 
The inclusion of ancestral state estimations in calculation of disparity under Brownian motion were more accurate than excluding ancestral states across all fossil sampling levels and disparity metrics.
This is intuitive, since ancestral state estimation of continuous characters uses the BM model as an assumption for the evolution of traits.
The assumptions of BM include a fixed mean and trait variance increasing linearly through time \citep{felsenstein1985phylogenies,omeara2006testing}.
We found that when these assumptions of BM were violated, such as under BM + trend and OU models, disparity errors returned by methods of \ase increased.
For example, under low/absent fossil sampling, methods of \ase performed worse than bypassing ancestral state estimation for BM + trend.
In this case, disparity estimates tended to be underestimated, since state estimations will be intermediates of the observed tip data, absent to the directional trend that occurred and thus missing past diversity.
This finding supports previous work that highlights the critical impact of fossil inclusion when directional trends are present in the trait data \citep{finarelli2006ancestral,slater2012integrating,finarelli2013potential}.
We found that there is a crossover between 5-15\% fossil sampling where \ase becomes optimal, which would equate to a densely sampled empirical phylogeny (\autoref{fig:continuous heatmap}).
While not tested in this study, we can also assume that disparity metrics that examine changes in position, such as average displacement \citep{guillerme2020shifting}, would be vastly underestimated without sufficient fossil sampling.



Similarly, calculations of disparity derived from OU simulations were also underestimated when using ancestral state estimations.
The OU process is characterised by a stationary variance, which contrasts the BM assumption of an increase in variance through time.
Therefore, since BM estimates internal nodes as a weighted average of the observed tip data, the ancestral nodes are compressed towards the centroid of the trait space.
This explains why, even with 100\% fossil sampling, bypassing \ase always outperformed the use of ancestral state estimation, since the fundamental model of estimation was incorrect.
However, in the case of the OU-derived disparity, disparity values returned from \ase were not necessarily far off those that bypassed ancestral state estimation, so the decision whether to use \ase in this scenario is a trade-off between the slight decreased accuracy of estimations versus the advantages of incorporating ancestral states in disparity analyses.

Previously, the choice of disparity metric has been probed as a critical step in the disparity pipeline, specifically for examining different aspects of trait space \citep{guillerme2020shifting}.
Indeed, our results do exhibit significant differences in disparity error between different metrics.
For example, recovering the sum of quantiles was ultimately harder than recovering the sum of variances or mean pairwise distance when using discrete traits.
However, the hierarchy of methods was relatively consistent across disparity metrics, suggesting that the relative performance of ancestral state estimation methods is robust to different disparity metrics.
Consistent performance across multiple disparity metrics can reassure us that multiple aspects of the true trait space are recovered, including size, dispersion and density.
Where there is inconsistency  in methods across metrics, then we can infer that the true disparity value has been attained artificially, rather than the recovery of a true trait space pattern.
This can be observed in the post-ordination distribution estimation of discrete traits, which performed well under the sum of variances and mean pairwise distance metrics, but noticeably worse under sum of quantiles.
